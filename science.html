Chapter 01 — Biochemistry

The Science of Ketosis

Ketosis is a metabolic state where your liver converts fatty acids into ketone bodies — clean-burning molecules that fuel your brain, muscles, and nearly every mitochondria-containing cell in the absence of sufficient glucose.

The Metabolic Switch

How your body swaps fuel systems

The transition happens over days. These are the four phases every keto dieter's body passes through.

01
Day 0–2

Carbs drop below 50g

Restricting carbohydrates depletes liver glycogen within 24–48 hours. Blood glucose falls; the body begins signalling for alternative fuel.

02
Day 2–4

Insulin plummets

Low insulin unlocks adipose tissue. Hormone-sensitive lipase releases free fatty acids into circulation at accelerated rates.

03
Day 3–7

Liver produces ketones

Hepatocyte mitochondria β-oxidize fatty acids into acetyl-CoA, then condense them into ketone bodies via HMG-CoA synthase.

04
Week 2–4

Fat adaptation

Tissues upregulate monocarboxylate transporters (MCT1/2) and ketolytic enzymes. You're now efficiently fat-fueled.

Ketone Bodies

The three molecules that replace glucose

During ketogenesis, the liver produces three distinct ketone bodies. Each has a specific role in cellular energy metabolism.

  • BHB
    β-HydroxybutyratePrimary blood ketone. Crosses the blood-brain barrier. What ketone meters measure.
  • AcAc
    AcetoacetateFirst ketone made. Detected by urine strips. Converted to BHB in tissues.
  • C₃H₆O
    AcetoneVolatile byproduct. Exhaled through breath — the source of "keto breath."
Cellular Impact

Why ketones are cleaner fuel

More ATP per oxygen

BHB produces more ATP per unit of oxygen than glucose — a more efficient cellular energy yield, especially under stress.

Less oxidative stress

Ketone metabolism generates fewer reactive oxygen species (ROS) than glucose metabolism — reduced cellular damage over time.

Signaling molecule

BHB is more than fuel — it's an HDAC inhibitor that upregulates genes involved in longevity, stress resistance, and antioxidant defense.

Brain preferred substrate

Under ketosis, the brain derives up to 70% of its energy from ketones — particularly protective in neurodegenerative conditions.
